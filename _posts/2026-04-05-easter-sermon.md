*April 2026*

The below post is an outline for an Easter sermon on Mark 15:40–16:8.

---

Welcome everyone. It's great to see you. It's wonderful to celebrate Easter together. What I want to do with you all over the next twenty minutes or so is to explore the meaning of Easter.

Easter takes us right into the heart of the deepest topic there is, which is the topic of life and death. Most people don't like talking about death—but Easter brings us face to face with our fear of death, the sting of death, and the one who conquered death for us.

## The fear of death

I don't know to what extent you've come across death in your own life. I used to work in marketing at a small agency in the city. We had a marketing team and a sales team, both led by these real charasmatic guys, I think in their sixties. One of the guys—his name was Alex—was a gifted salesman. He led the sales team and hammered on about hitting the targets each quarter. He thought in quarters, you see—three month blocks. And then one day he got a cancer diagnosis—it was already significantly developed—and he died within six months. The marketing guy—his name was Rick—was larger than life. He was my direct manager and a real friend of mine. He loved life. He was a bit different to the other guy in that he didn't live for his work; he lived for the weekend. He seemed to only ever think one week at a time. He loved Friday beers. He loved Saturday nights out. He rented this place in Mosman—never bought. One year, maybe on a whim, he went on this scuba diving holiday, and during one of the dives he descended too deep too quickly. He passed out and died.

Now if you had asked these guys during their life if they feared death, I'm pretty sure they would say no. Knowing Rick, he might have quoted someone like Mark Twain who said : “I do not fear death. I was dead for billions and billions of years before I was born, and I didn't suffer the slightest inconvenience from it.”

Maybe that's how you feel about death. It's not on your mind much. It's certainly not something you actively fear when you think about your week, or your next three months.

But I want to suggest that the reason these guys didn't seem to fear death, and the reason you might not live with a visceral fear of death at the forefront of your minds, is not because there isn't a sense of the gravity of death in you somewhere, it's that we all do our best to put it aside and push it down.

I had a friend who had something wrong with his car and it made this rattling noise. The noise was a constant reminder that something was deeply wrong with his car. But he was clever, my friend. What he did was as the rattle got louder, he just turned the music up.

We do something similar. When the fear of death starts to rattle us, starts to make some noise in our souls, we are pretty quick to turn up the music of our lives—our careers, our money, our homes, our social lives, our hobbies, our entertainment, our weekends and quarterly goals. We turn them up to drown out the rattle.

Easter is a chance to turn the music down and face the rattle head on.

The Bible says: ***"Death is the destiny of everyone; the living should take this to heart."* (Ecclesiastes 7:2).**

When is the best time to take death to heart? Well, there's no good time, is there? You don't want to talk about death when you're out on a date. You don't want to talk about death on your Zoom call while you're waiting for others to join the call. There is rarely a convenient time to think about death. All we know for sure is that the best time to think about death is while we're still alive.

So let's do it now. Let's do it with the help of the Bible. Christians believe the Bible is God's word and gives us divine insight into life and death—it certainly brings clarity to it. I want to show you some verses from the Bible that bring clarity.

One verse I want to show you comes from Jesus' own brother, James, who wrote this: ***"What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes."*(James 4:14)**. When you realise that death is not long off for any of us, that brings clarity to life. Most peoples' experience of life is not that time slows down as you get older but that it actually seems to go by faster. I turned 31 last week and I said to my wife that I'm probably about a third of the way through life and she said it might be closer to half—a sobering moment! As a pastor I get people asking me about life and death and one question that sometimes comes up is people ask if it is true that right before you die, your life flashes before your eyes. And my standard response is, yes, that does happen—it's called life! Life goes by so quickly that by the time you're on your deathbed it'll have felt like a flash before your eyes.

And you might think that I'm just trying to afraid. You might think I'm just trying to sting you by showing you how short life can be. But what I really want to show people is that the sting of death is not that it comes so soon. The real sting of death is not that even though you might be really successful in your career, chances are your reputation will be forgotten within a lifetime, and any effort you make to nudge the world in the right direction will very likely be undone too. The real sting of death is not that it means even your best attempts at happiness are short-lived, or that it raises all sorts of doubts about the meaning of life now.

There is a verse in the Bible that tells us what the real sting of death is. It's written by the Apostle Paul. He says: ***"The sting of death is sin."* (1 Corinthians 15:56)**.

Sin is a word that describes a spiritual reality that exists between us and God. If you don't believe in God, suspend your disbelief for a moment to get this idea. Assume for a moment that there is a creator, a God of all. If God created humanity for a purpose and with a way to be, and we as human beings reject that way, ignore that purpose and rebel against God, what happens? The Bible says we are all in this predicament. We have all sinned against God by rebelling, ignoring, rejecting Him. The Bible says that when we reject God as the author of life, we are left with death. More than that, we face any justice or judgement that is due to us because of our sin. The Bible says that the spiritual crime of rejecting our God carries an eternal penalty—we become subject to death and eternal judgement.

If this is true, then the real sting of death—the real reason why our fear of death is valid—is because we are all guilty of sin.

If our rejection of God leads to God's rejection of us, then there is no hope. But the Bible says that there is hope, because though we reject God, he does not reject us—he reaches out in human history to demonstrate his reality and to offer us reconciliation.

## The one who conquered death

During his life Jesus called people to believe in him and follow him as the one person who could reconcile people and God.

How does Jesus reconcile people and God? A few things you need to know.

The Bible says that Jesus was without sin. Unlike anyone else in all human history, Jesus perfectly obeyed God the Father.

The Bible portrays Jesus as both fully man and fully God; one suitable to bring the two together.

The Bible says that Jesus' death was a sacrificial death. On the one hand it was an event which accorded with the plot of people who hated Jesus. On the other hand it was an event which accorded with the plan of God to demonstrate His love for people.

The Bible says that Jesus died "for our sins". He died to take on himself a penalty for a crime he didn't commit—a crime that everyone else commits—a spiritual crime against God.

He took on himself the sin of the world and died so that our sins might be wiped away and the penalty of sin with it.

Now—this is a great spiritual claim, that Jesus can wipe our sin away. The problem with spiritual claims, though, is that they are just that—spiritual. Invisible. Hard to test. God might have done something spiritually significant through Jesus' death but how can we even begin to assess whether there is any reality to it?

Well, let me ask you this. If someone goes to jail as penalty for a crime—how do we know the penalty has been paid and the judge satisfied? When the jail doors are open, and the prisoner goes free.

Jesus went to the place of judgement for us. He went to death. And though locked in his tomb by a large stone, on the third day he walked free, because the penalty had been paid and the judge satisfied. Now this is not just a spiritual claim—it is an historical one. One we can get our hands on.

We read the account in Mark's gospel. The women came to the tomb, and from Mark 16 verse 4 it says:

But when they looked up, they saw that the stone, which was very large, had been rolled away. As they entered the tomb, they saw a young man dressed in a white robe sitting on the right side, and they were alarmed. “Don’t be alarmed,” he said. “You are looking for Jesus the Nazarene, who was crucified. He has risen! He is not here. See the place where they laid him. But go, tell his disciples and Peter, ‘He is going ahead of you into Galilee. There you will see him, just as he told you.’” (Mark 16:4–7).

And here is the final verse in the whole book of Mark's gospel—verse 8: ***"Trembling and bewildered, the women went out and fled from the tomb. They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid."* (Mark 16:8)**.

The earliest manuscripts we have of Mark's gospel end the story there, like that. You might wonder why. I do too.

Here's what I think is going on. Mark wrote this account of Jesus' life and by this point through the teachings and miracles of Jesus he has demonstrated what he said about Jesus in the very first verse of the book—that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God. Mark is likely writing at a time decades after Jesus' death so that the claim of his resurrection is already widely known. Everyone already knows about the resurrection. They know the claims—you probably know them too—that Jesus rose from the tomb, he wasn't there, but he eventually appeared to people—Paul says he appeared to hundreds of people—before ascending back into the heavens. It's been debated, talked about, and the church is already growing with some speed. Mark is writing this whole account to give us a punchy, clear basis from which to assess the resurrection.

It ends on a note of fear, and rightly so. Because if the resurrection is true, it's scary. If there is more to life, that turns everything upside down for those of us who assumed there wasn't.

The way you view death will transform the way you view life. If you think death is the full-stop, your life sentence can contain whatever you want it to contain—it doesn't really matter. But if death is a comma into something else, and that something else is very much shaped by the life we lead now, then all of a sudden life is very important.

If Jesus' death really was for sin, as a demonstration of God's love and willingness to forgive us, and if we then reject Jesus and the love of God—we miss out on reconciliation, we miss out on life, we miss out on God, we remain under God's right judgement.

So the call to action, so to speak, from what God has done in history is to put your faith—your trust—in Jesus. Entrust your life to Him. Pray and ask his forgiveness. And then walk in newness of life.

Because while the news of the resurrection is scary—it's good news for those who trust in Jesus. It means eternal life. And eternal life is not just something we look forward to—though we do. It's something we enjoy right now.

## Scary good news in the face of death

How does the news of the resurrection actually impact life right here and now? Well, I think there's one major way—it gives us confidence in the face of death to live life to the full.

Earlier this year I was one of millions of people worldwide who watched the same thing on Netflix in the same week—it was this 90-minute long thing of a man named Alex Honnold climbing this building called Taipei 101. What made it fascinating was that he did it all without ropes or safety equipment. Alex Honnold has been doing this for his whole life—climbing, taking risks, living on the edge. So much so that a few years back scientists wanted to study him. They did an MRI scan of him and looked closely at his amygdala—the fear centre of the brain—and they realised something. It wasn't firing as it was supposed to. Alex wasn't experiencing fear the way people are generally meant to. All because there is something fundamentally different going on inside him.

When you look at the disciples of Jesus, you notice something strange. Before Jesus' death they were full of fear and would do anything to stave off harm to themselves, and yet after Jesus' died they were full of courage and would do anything to spread the news of Jesus to anyone who would listen. So much so that it's as if something fundamentally changed within them. Their brains and their hearts had experienced some deep transformation which meant they no longer feared death. The change that happened was that they had witnessed the resurrection of Jesus and they therefore knew with confidence for the first time that death is not the end.

And for Jesus' disciples today—those who know Him and follow Him—we can live radically new lives if we have experienced that inner change. We can be radically generous with what we have, we can hold radically loosely to the things of this world, we can give up our own interests in the pursuit of the good of others, we can put aside the burden of careerism, the burden of having to win some great victory for humanity in our lifetimes, we can put aside the fear of missing out on some great achievement in our lifetime, because we know that this life is not all there is; in fact, it's just the beginning.

Let me pray.

Heavenly Father, we thank you for Easter and the opportunity it presents to turn down the noise of life and reflect on what truly matters. We thank you that because of who Jesus is and what He did in his death and resurrection, we can have real hope in the face of death, and we can enjoy reconciliation with you, forgiveness of our sin, and life to the full. We pray that we would entrust our lives to Jesus. In his name we pray, amen.

C. S. Lewis penned a brief essay, “On Living in an Atomic Age,” in 1948, when the possibility of a nuclear war loomed. He wrote that many people were frightened that the atomic bomb could “totally destroy civilization itself.” He responded: “What were your views about the ultimate future of civilization before the atomic bomb …? Of course, audiences are meant to laugh at this point in the movie, but Greene won’t let us off the hook. He believes the point is no laughing matter. He reasons that, if you knew you were going to die tomorrow, then doing your homework would certainly be pointless. And if we knew that the world was about to be incinerated, it would make all the things we think of as so significant—art, politics, rearing a family—pointless and futile.
