# Notes

Add 1 Cor 10.11-14 to exodus sermon on betrayal

Contrast autonomy and gods law in exodus account

The human heart is an idol factory.

There was a terrible school that taught its students to play basketball by giving them books to read on how to play basketball. Once the students had finished their reading, the teachers were content that they had learned to play. How silly! And yet this is sometimes our approach to discipleship. We assume that by reading the Bible, we are learning to follow Jesus. But we are only learning the theory of discipleship. To become basketball players, we must play basketball. To become Jesus' followers, we must follow Jesus.

One of the greatest idols of our culture is the child. Abraham demonstrated faith by willing to sacrifice his child; though God didn’t let him in the end. Abraham did it not because he thought God wanted his son dead; he did it because God had power to raise the dead. When God sent forth his son however, his son did die—because God loves people. His son didn’t stay dead; brought about resurrection.


Someone said australia had built an economy on shipping rocks overseas and speculating on shelter — home ownership. I feel nervous broaching this topic, on the one hand because I don’t own a home and open myself up to charges of naivety or jealousy, but I mainly feel nervous because I feel I am attacking something sacred — the great sacred Australian cow. Perhaps the saying we find most uncomfortable is not about hatred or lust or judgement or hell — but rather the saying “birds have nests, foxes have holes, but the son of man is not a homeowner”

Israel are out of Egypt, but Egypt isn’t completely out of them.

Lately in a wreck of a Californian ship, one of the passengers fastened a belt about him with two hundred pounds of gold in it, with which he was found afterwards at the bottom. Now, as he was sinking—had he the gold? or the gold him?
