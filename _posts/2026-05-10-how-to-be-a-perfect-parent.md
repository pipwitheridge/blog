## The question all parents want an answer to

If there's one thing kids do better at than adults, it's asking questions. "Why is the sky blue?", "Why do I have to go to sleep?", "Why can't I have ice cream for dinner?"—the list goes on. And usually, just when you think you've heard them all, your child asks a question that sends your brain spinning for a response.

"What happens when I die?" is one of those questions. What do you say?

That's a tough, profound question, and it's not the only one. Questions about life, faith, relationships—these can be tough to know how to handle.

Kids have lots of questions, but sometimes mums and dads just have one: "Am I a good parent?"

It's a question all parents want an answer to. It's a question that keeps some of us up at night. We worry: Am I doing this right? Will my kids turn out okay? Am I giving them the best possible education? Are they making enough friends? Are they making the *right* friends? Is my child going to be alright?

If you've asked yourself these questions, you're not alone. And here's the good news: you don't have to figure it out by yourself. Christians believe that God wants to help parents in raising their children, because God loves people (including parents and their children!). If you are not a Christian, my encouragement is to be open to the wisdom the Bible gives. Let's keep exploring.

## What the Bible says about parenthood

The Bible has lots to say about parenthood: the good, the bad, and the ugly. It tells stories of parents who got it right, parents who got it spectacularly wrong, and everything in between. You'll find parents wrestling with the same fears and joys that you do today—wanting the best for their kids, making mistakes, trying again, discovering that their children teach them as much as they teach them.

Take for example the story of Jacob, found in the very first book of the Bible. Jacob favored one of his sons (Joseph) over his other sons, which created devastating family conflict. The brothers fought with one another, and sent Joseph away. But in God's timing, the brothers were reunited and reconciled. Jacob called his sons together and offered blessings to each of them. It's a mixed story—a rollercoaster of emotions—which holds a mirror up to our own failures and successes as parents.

There is another part of the Bible which offers short snippets of wisdom for life: the book of Proverbs. Each Proverb offers not an exact rule for life, but rather a guiding principle. Proverbs 22:6 says: "Train up a child in the way they should go; even when they are old, they will not depart from it." This is a general truth: when we offer wise training, tutoring, and guidance to help our kids in the way they should go, the benefits unfold even into their adult lives.

But here's a devastating truth: no matter how good we are as parents, we'll never be perfect—we all make mistakes. We will say things, do things, and think things that fall far short of what "good" looks like. We might get snappy when we should be patient, we might forget important tasks, we might put too much pressure on our kids. We will say the wrong words, we will think the wrong things, we *will* make mistakes. It is inevitable, because none of us are perfect.

Yet the Bible reveals to us one parent who is perfect—only one. If we want to grow as parents, we must take a look at the one perfect parent.

## The perfect parent

The Bible gives us lots of examples of parents. But there's only one parent in the Bible described as being completely perfect. The perfect parent is not a human parent—it's God!

Now, you might be thinking: *Wait, God as a parent? How does that help me with my actual kids?* Stick with me.

God is described in the Bible as "Our Father in heaven." Throughout the Bible, God acts like a parent: he provides, he guides, he disciplines with love, and he never stops caring even when his children mess up.

God warns his people about danger. He gives them instruction on how to live. He explains consequences and yet welcomes his people back into his arms even after they disobey him. The prophet Isaiah describes it beautifully: God carries his people like a parent carries a small child, close to His heart.

We see this perhaps most clearly in the famous parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15:11–32). In this story, a young man asks his father for his inheritance early and squanders it all in reckless living. Broken and desperate, he returns home, expecting rejection. But his father sees him from afar and runs to embrace him with overwhelming joy. There are no harsh words, only celebration and forgiveness.

God is that father, and we are that son. Even though we so often ignore God while squandering the good gifts he gives us, his heart aches for us. He welcomes us home not with judgment but with open arms.

When you look at how God relates to His people—with patience, with forgiveness, with a kind of love that doesn't depend on performance—you get a glimpse of what perfect parenting actually looks like.


## Hope for imperfect parents

I love my daughter, but I know I am not a perfect dad. When I think about the mistakes I've already made as a parent, and when I think ahead to all the potential mistakes I'll make as my daughter grows up, it's easy for me to feel despair. And yet, my despair turns to joy when I realise that God cares for me more than I could possibly dream. God cares for me like a child. 1 John 3:1 says, "Behold what manner of love the Father has bestowed on us, that we should be called children of God!"

It is liberating to know that God's love for me doesn't depend on my performance as a parent. It also gives me deep comfort to know that God wants to help me as a parent. If you feel like an imperfect parent, it's probably because you are—in fact, we all are!

The goal of parenthood isn't to be perfect—and it's not even to raise perfect kids. The goal of parenthood is to raise our kids so that they know that even though they are not perfect, and even though we are not perfect, we have a God who loves us perfectly.
