## Introduction

==New Series==
Today we begin a new series in the second half of Exodus. There are lots of reasons to study Exodus.

==Non-b,GrtStry==
Even people who don't believe any of it take interest in it. Hollywood has made movies about it. That God would rescue a nation of slaves by sending plagues on their captors and then splitting the great Red Sea in two so that the slaves could walk on dry land to freedom—that is a great story in its own right.

==B,OurStry==
But for the believer it takes on another level. Because not only is it a good story; for the Christian, it is also our story, and it's a window into the character of God and how He works to save not just Israel, but all people. Ask any Christian about their journey with God and they could tell you the Exodus story: I was a slave, God won a great victory for me over that which kept me captive, and I was rescued by God's power and mercy. My chains are gone; I've been set free. Not only that; God equips me by His word to live His way while I wander in the wilderness of life and look forward to what's ahead. So, if you want to understand God better, and if you want to understand your own life better, study the book of Exodus.

==StrtingEx19,JrneyToFrdm==
This term we're studying the second half of the book of Exodus, from chapters 19 onward. You might wonder why we even need the second half of Exodus. Does the story really lose anything if you just take it out? Wouldn't it have been a lot simpler and better if God brought Israel out of Egypt and then let them straight in the promised land? Well, what we see through studying the second half of Exodus is that God wants more than just to re-pot the nation of Israel. He wants a relationship with Israel built on trust and worship. We've seen the journey out of slavery, now we come to the journey to freedom. And the journey to true freedom involves getting to know God and His commands and His covenant before entering the promised land. It involves God giving His people words to live by before they enter the land. And in Exodus 19 where we pick up from today, we see God speak to his people at Mount Sinai.

## 1. Rescued by God

==Ex19:1-4-...==
Read with me from Exodus 19:1:
***"On the first day of the third month after the Israelites left Egypt—on that very day—they came to the Desert of Sinai. After they set out from Rephidim, they entered the Desert of Sinai, and Israel camped there in the desert in front of the mountain. Then Moses went up to God, and the Lord called to him from the mountain and said, “This is what you are to say to the descendants of Jacob and what you are to tell the people of Israel: ‘You yourselves have seen what I did to Egypt, and how I carried you on eagles’ wings and brought you to myself."* Exodus 19:1–4**

==GdRscd,Carried,GdChsWeakMksStrong==
Let's pause there. Part of the way God works in the world is He chooses that which is weak, and makes it strong. The reason God does this is so that people can see with no mistake that God is in control, He's good, and He's powerful.

==ILL:football,principal,interiordesigner==
If you want to demonstrate your skill as a football player, join the worst team and win them the championship. If you want to demonstrate your skills as a principal, pick a struggling school and turn it around so it tops the state. If you want to demonstrate your skills as an interior designer, go down into the den and make it look nice. Choose what is weak and do something unbelievable with it. That's what God does. That's how he demonstrates his power.

==GodChsAbraham(Old),Moses(speaker),David(Young),12Discples,You/Me,Israel==
He chose Moses, though Moses was not good speaker;  He chose twelve disciples who were altogether unimpressive; He chooses you and me if we trust Him, though we are nothing special; and He chose Israel, though they were small and in slavery.

==QT: Bray-...==
British Theologian Gerald Bray wrote a book called ‘God is Love’, and in it he said this: “Those who study the ancient world from a secular point of view ... will examine the great civilizations of Assyria and Egypt, of Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome, but Israel (if it figures at all) will hardly be more than a blip on their mental horizon. To write the history of the ancient world putting Israel at the centre is rather like writing the history of Europe from the standpoint of Luxembourg, a country that is geographically central but otherwise insignificant. Yet that is precisely what the Bible does, and the truth of its claim has been borne out by events. It is no exaggeration to say that while the great empires of the past have disappeared into history, the tiny nation of Israel still exists and has spread its wings to the far corners of the globe ... All over the world, children are still named David and Rebecca, but not Nebuchadnezzar.”

==IsrlDidntEarnRescue==
God chose what was weak and made it strong. Israel did not earn their escape from Egypt; they did not strive for it. It was completely an act of God; it is as if they were carried out on Eagle's wings.

==ILL:POVIsrliteDuringExo,how,IDontKnow!GodDidIt==
Imagine you were an Israelite at the time of the Exodus. You wake up in the morning and you look forward to twelve hours of forced labour. You have to put up with the slave drivers barking at you to make bricks without straw. Your hands are tired. Your hope is fading. Then one day, you notice a bunch of frogs jumping from the Nile river to the king's palace. Then a few days later, you overhear one of the Egyptian slave drivers complaining about all the flies in his house. A few days later you get a day off because all the slave drivers are home sick with boils. Then they need to take extended leave because hail has rained down and damaged their cars—their chariots. And then one day, you get the word. It's go time. It's time to leave the chains behind and leave Egypt behind.
You turn to your buddy next to you. He asks you "where are we going?"
"I don't know." You reply.
He asks you, "How will we get there?"
"I don't know." You reply. But this Moses fellow seems to know the way.
"Are you sure he knows the way? He's taking us to the sea."
"Well, let's just wait and find out."
And before long you're on the other side of the Red Sea, out of Egypt, you turn around and the Sea is parted in two, and your buddy asks, "how did that happen?"
And you say, "I don't know but I didn't have anything to do with it."

==HowYouBcChristian?IDon'tKnow!GodDidIt==
You ask me how I became a Christian and one one level it's like, "I don't know—it wasn't me." God gifted me a Christian family. God brought me to a church that nurtured me. God at some point put it in my heart that I'm a sinner, and then quickly showed me that Jesus is my saviour. God carried me. One day you are a slave to sin and a slave to the fear of death. And then God sets you free. He carries you on Eagles wings. He gives you a new heart, a new mind. The Spirit of God works through the word of God to introduce you to the son of God who died and rose again. If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed (John 8:36). He frees those who were once held as slaves by the fear of death (Hebrews 2:15). It is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourself, it is the gift of God (Ephesians 2).

## 2. Set apart for God

==NotJstRecue,Relationship==
Israel were rescued by God. But God wasn't simply interested in rescuing them. He rescues them for a purpose. And that purpose is to enter into a covenant relationship with Him. God rescues them and sets them apart for Himself.

==vv4-6==
Read with me again from the start of verse 4, God's words to Israel through Moses:
***‘You yourselves have seen what I did to Egypt, and how I carried you on eagles’ wings and brought you to myself. Now if you obey me fully and keep my covenant, then out of all nations you will be my treasured possession. Although the whole earth is mine, you will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation.’ These are the words you are to speak to the Israelites.”* (Exodus 19:4–6).**

==Alleviate,Elevate,KngdmPrsts,HolyNatn==
God alleviates Israel's suffering by freeing them from slavery, then He elevates them from slavery to royalty: a kingdom of priests and a holy nation.

==Holy=Special/SetApart,Stigma,Good=NthingSpcial==
The word "holy" has a stigma to it. We don't like the person who is "holier-than-thou".

==Key=chosnNotBecauseHoly,HolyBecauseChosenByGrace==
So why can Israel call themself holy? Are they really holy? Are they really special? And here's the key to understanding holiness: God doesn't choose them because they are special; they are special because God chooses them. They are set apart for God; given a special purpose in the world. Not because they are strong or deserving; quite the opposite—because they are weak and undeserving.

==KingdomOfPriests=>Priests=AccessToGod,KoP=EveryoneHasAccess==
 The phrase "kingdom of priests" is a rich one. A priest is one with access to God. A kingdom of priests is a nation of people who each have direct access to God. Israel came to have this access through the tabernacle which they would come to for worship.

==Priest=enablesAccess,DivineVocation==
But a priest is also someone who enables others to access God. Israel were to be a light shining in the darkness of the world, that the nations would come to know God and enter into the fold of God's family. Israel had a divine calling, what we might call a vocation, to bring the message of God to the world.

==ModernConfusionOfIsraelVocation==
You might wrestle with this question as well. Whether or not you are aware of it, this question of Israel's place in the world is ablaze in many parts of the church today in light of the current Israel–Iran war. It has many Christian commentators speculating and debating Israel's place in our world today. I want to say two things on this topic, briefly.

==CannotAlienateIsrael==
First, we cannot alienate Israel in our thinking. All Israelis and Jews are deserving of dignity and respect, because all people are made in the image of God. The violence and vitriol against Jews in the twentieth century, and in our century, was and is evil. The Bondi Attacks were evil. And we mourn with all those in mourning over those attacks.

==CannotReadModernIsraelIntoAncientPromises==
Secondly, the promises to Israel in the Bible find their fulfilment in Jesus Christ. Jesus transforms worship so that it is no longer tied to geography but goes to the ends of the Earth. The New Testament shows no concern for securing the geographic bounds of God's people through military might. Jesus and Paul are both very clear that the new people of God in the New Testament are marked not by physical descent from Abraham, but by faith in God, the same sort of faith that Abraham had.

==PaulComplaintFulfilmentOfIsraelVocation==
Israel struggled with this vocation. The apostle Paul was sad that Israel had failed in this vocation when their Messiah came. Paul saw Himself as one of a minority of God's people who actually lived out the vocation they had been given in spreading the good news of the Messiah, while much of Israel rejected Him. Paul was trying to wrestle with Israel's place in a world where the Messiah had come and gone.

==WeSetApartInChrist,CallToObey,NotMosesButChrist==
We too have been set apart by God for God. Just as Israel were called to obey God fully, so we are called obey God fully. Israel came under the law of Moses, which we'll look at next week when we look at the Ten Commandments. But we Christians are not under the law of Moses, we are under the law of Christ. That is, we seek to obey Jesus fully.

==StillPartOfObedienceIsFullfillingVocationOfPriests==
Part of what it means to obey Jesus is to declare the praises of God who has shown us incredible mercy despite our sin. The apostle Peter writes to Christians, ***"But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God’s special possession, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy."* (1 Peter 2:9–10).**

==PartOfObedienceIsLivingInNewIdentity,ILL: PrisonerWhoCantAdjustToFreedom==
Coming out of slavery to sin and entering into a royal priesthood of God's people is not always as easy. Getting a new identiy is not straightforward. In the Shawshank Redemption, there's a prisoner called Red who after decades of being in prison gets his freedom. He goes back into society as a free man but he cannot help but act like he's still in jail. He gets a job and his boss gets annoyed at him because he keeps asking permission to use the bathroom. He's acting like he's in prison. When we're moved from slavery to sin to freedom in Christ we might be out of prison but we sometimes act like we're still there. We fall back into sin, we forget that we were released from it. Part of growing as a Christian is learning to live in accordance with our newfound freedom. To live as one who has been rescued by God, and now has access to God.

Being a priest of God is an enormous priviledge and it carries a weighty responsibility. We each have access to God through Jesus. And Jesus calls on his people to be a channel of God's mercy to the nations, declaring his praises wherever we find ourselves.

Artemis mission

Contrary to Roman Catholicism which teaches the priesthood of the few, the Bible paints us a picture of a royal priesthood of all believers. The task of priests today is not to perform sacrifices for the people, but to proclaim the sacrifice Jesus made in his death.

It is an enormous priveledge we each have. And it transforms the way we view ourselves. People today define themselves by their career, their job, where they live, their hobbies. The Christian defines who they are in terms of whose they are. I am a child of God.

You might be a teacher, a home-owner, a Sydney-sider, an avid runner or reader or gardener or whatever. But if you're a Christian, you are God's treasured possession.

This should transform the way we live, and it should transform the way others view our lives. If we live with a visceral sense of being a child of God, one should be able to look at us and say, "I can see you studying diligently, but I know your identity is not bound up in your studies ... I can see you working honourably, but I know you are not living for your work ... I can see you are wise with your money, but I know that money is not your big 'why' .... I can see you are enjoying retirement, but I know you have not resigned yourself from the work God has given you to do in life ... I can see you love your family as a parent or brother or sister, but your primary joy is in being a child of God". Of course our friends and family who are not believers themselves might not put it that way, but when our identity is truly rooted in who God is and what Jesus has done, there should nevertheless be this sense in them around us that though we might not be anything special, yet there is something different about us.

Having your identity in Christ shapes over time the way people view you; it also transforms the way we see ourselves. When we stuff up, we know we are not a failure, we are someone whom God treasures and forgives. When we suffer loss of reputation or income, we know that we are of far more worth than we feel and can even imagine, because God has purchased us for Himself at an incredible price. In other words, having your identity in Christ rescues us from despair. But it also rescues us from pride, because when we succeed, we still are able to realise that we are not self-sufficient; we are someone whom God carries day by day on Eagles wings.


# # 3. Waiting to approach God

Israel were rescued by God, set apart by God, and now, at the foot of Mount Sinai, they wait to approach God. And it is here where we get a window into some of the fundamental differences between Israel's story and our story. For though we approach God with freedom through Christ, Israel's experience of God was different. From verse 16 it says:

***"On the morning of the third day there was thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain, and a very loud trumpet blast. Everyone in the camp trembled. Then Moses led the people out of the camp to meet with God, and they stood at the foot of the mountain. Mount Sinai was covered with smoke, because the Lord descended on it in fire. The smoke billowed up from it like smoke from a furnace, and the whole mountain trembled violently. As the sound of the trumpet grew louder and louder, Moses spoke and the voice of God answered him. The Lord descended to the top of Mount Sinai and called Moses to the top of the mountain. So Moses went up and the Lord said to him, “Go down and warn the people so they do not force their way through to see the Lord and many of them perish. Even the priests, who approach the Lord, must consecrate themselves, or the Lord will break out against them.”* (Exodus 19:16–22).**

The Israelites' experience of God was marked by fear and trembling. As Christians we are called to fear God, but we're not called to be afraid of God—and this is something of the sense we get in Exodus. Their fear seems more like being afraid. That's because God's holiness did not allow them to come any closer. Because their holiness did not match God's.

But in the New Testament, when Jesus dies for sins, he gives us His holiness, which is God's holiness—his righteousness. What that means is that Israel's approach to God was very different to the Christian approach to God. Jesus makes all the difference.

This is what the author of Hebrews says. He talks about the mountain experience in Exodus 19 and says this is not the Christian experience. Our approach is very different. In Hebrews 12,

***"You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: “If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death.” The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, “I am trembling with fear.” But you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant...".* (Hebrews 12:18–24).**

Maybe your experience of approaching God has felt more like that first mountain, and it's looked more like the experience of Israel at Mount Sinai. Maybe your experience has been one of fear, uncertainty. Maybe your view of God therefore has been of a God who is distant, a God who you cannot get close to and who will not draw close to you.

In Jesus, we are able to come to the very presence of God.

I said at the start that Exodus is Israel's story, it's our story, and it's God's story. All those come together in the life of Jesus.

The life of Jesus is the fulfilment of Israel's story. Exodus begins with a child being born, a tyrant who threatens his death, an escape from Egypt, a passing through the waters, a testing in the wilderness, the giving of the law on a mountain, and the formation of a new people of God. In the Gospel of Matthew, Jesus is born, a tyrant threatens his death, he escapes to Egypt and then comes out, he passes through the water of John's baptism, he is tested in the wilderness by the devil, he delivers the law afresh in his sermon on the mount, and then he forms a new people—he calls his twelve disciples.

As we continue to study the book of Exodus, the difference between Israel's story and our story—the difference between these two mountains—will become clearer. But let me encourage you: the more we understand Israel's story, the more we'll understand our own. The more we see God at work in rescuing and restoring and equipping Israel as they look to the promised land, the more we'll understand what God has done in rescuing us and restoring us and equipping us for life as we look to the future hope He has promised.

***For now, in Jesus and through faith in Jesus we may approach God with freedom and confidence.* (Eph 3:12)**.

So let's pray to God.


# Notes

God comes amongst the people in the tabernacle; Moses goes before God on behalf of the people. Jesus is both God amongst mankind and mankind before God.

We have been made holy in one sense, and yet the call remains: be holy. What does this mean for the Christian?

Opportunity to counter the Roman Catholic (and Eastern Orthodox?) view of priesthood.

Compare Moses speaking on Mountain to Jesus.

God makes his people holy by dwelling among them; but it is not enough; He must dwell in them, and this is what happens in the New Testament.

CH Wright - To obey God’s voice and keep God’s covenant is expressed as a condition, not of gaining God’s redemption (for that has already happened) but of fulfilling the mission their identity lays on them. Identity and obedience flow from grace.

Israel are God's treasured possession. Things will only work out, though, if Israel treasures God above all else as well.

D. Alexander: "What God desires for Israelites in the Old Testament prefigures a greater spiritual exodus, involving all the nations of the world, that comes through Jesus Christ."

And if you’re not a Christian, it reveals what’s required — you are a slave, you need God’s help, the future hope can be yours.

Maybe your story has involved a journey out of slavery … slavery to addiction, slavery to fear of man … but on the deepest level we are all part of a journey out of slavery and a journey to freedom.
